# Website Audience Audit — Grassroots Hoppers

**Date:** March 6, 2026
**Scope:** Full site surface (index.html, learn.html, hackathon/)
**Method:** Read the entire site through six distinct audience lenses. For each: what they see, what's missing, what would make them say "hell yes."

---

## Audience 1: Social Investors & Impact Funds

**Who they are:** People at Ashoka, Sociale InnovatieFabriek, Belfius Smart Belgium, European Social Innovation Competition, King Baudouin Foundation, social enterprise accelerators. They evaluate initiatives for impact potential, scalability, financial sustainability, and team.

**What they see right now:**
- A clear thesis (AI-assisted development lowers the barrier, communities can own their tools)
- A real Belgian entity (GPFC srl, BCE number)
- Two projects in motion (David Toolkit, Social Media V2)
- Cooperative legal model mentioned
- A first hackathon announced

**What's missing — the hard blockers:**
1. **No impact metrics or theory of change.** Investors need to see: who benefits, how many, what changes, how you measure it. The site describes *what* GH does but never *what changes* because of it. No beneficiary numbers, no target KPIs, no impact framework.
2. **No financial model or sustainability signal.** "Contributors get ownership, not pay" is interesting but raises the question: how does this sustain itself? No mention of revenue model, grant strategy, or how GPFC srl stays solvent. An investor reads this and thinks: passion project, not investable entity.
3. **No team page.** One founder (Julien) is mentioned in passing. No bio, no LinkedIn, no background, no advisory board, no partners. Investors fund people, and the site has almost no information about who's behind this.
4. **No traction proof.** The external proof cards (Michał, Kyeyune, Mike) are about *other people* building software, not about GH's own traction. How many people have engaged? How many forks? How many communities interested? Zero numbers anywhere.
5. **No "ask."** The site never says what it needs. Investors want to know: are you looking for funding? A pilot partner? Mentoring? The CTA is "email Julien" — that's a chat, not a pitch.

**What would make them say "hell yes":**
- A one-paragraph impact thesis near the top: "We aim to help X communities in Y cities own their own software by Z date."
- A visible theory of change: problem → intervention → output → outcome → impact
- A "Support Us" or "Partner With Us" section with a clear ask (grant funding for pilot workshops, in-kind support, advisory seats)
- A team/about page showing who Julien is, what his background is, who advises
- 3-5 traction numbers (even small ones): repos created, workshop attendees, communities reached, GitHub stars

## Audience 2: Incubators & Accelerators

**Who they are:** Co.Station, Start it @KBC, iMinds/imec, BECI, hub.brussels, Impact Hub Brussels, European Social Enterprise programs. They look for: problem-solution fit, early traction, founder capability, market potential, coachability.

**What they see right now:**
- Strong problem framing (section 02, "The Shift")
- Analogy to Transition Towns — shows the founder thinks in frameworks
- Two concrete products
- AGPL licensing commitment (serious open-source intent)

**What's missing — the hard blockers:**
1. **No clear "problem → solution → why us" flow in the first 10 seconds.** An accelerator screener spends 30 seconds on a site. The hero says "We help communities create and govern their own software." That's a mission statement, not a problem. Where's the pain point stated as a sharp sentence?
2. **No competitive landscape.** The site doesn't acknowledge alternatives (Decidim, CoopCycle, Bonfire are mentioned as peers, not competitors). Incubators want to know: why is GH different from existing platforms? What's the defensible angle?
3. **No roadmap or milestones.** "Two projects, both open source" — but where are they in their lifecycle? What ships when? What's the 6-month plan? 12-month plan? No timeline anywhere.
4. **No user/customer signal.** Is anyone actually using David Toolkit? Has anyone expressed interest in Social Media V2? No testimonials, no waitlist, no "N people have signed up." The proof section shows other people building AI tools — that's market validation for AI, not for GH.
5. **The site reads as a manifesto, not a startup.** Accelerators want to see: we've validated X, we're building Y, we need Z to get to the next stage. The site tells a story but doesn't show a business trajectory.

**What would make them say "hell yes":**
- A sharp problem statement in the hero or immediately after: "Small businesses and communities are locked out of the tools that big corporations use. GH changes that."
- A "Where We Are" section: what's shipped, what's in progress, what's next (with dates)
- A competitive positioning statement: "Unlike X and Y, GH focuses on Z"
- Early traction signals: GitHub activity, first deployment, community interest
- A one-page pitch deck link (PDF) for download — accelerators love a structured deck

## Audience 3: Rob Hopkins & Transition Movement Peers

**Who they are:** Rob Hopkins himself, Transition Network staff, local Transition Town groups (1,400+ worldwide), resilience/relocalisation thinkers, commons-oriented activists. They care about: community empowerment, replicability, not reinventing the wheel, local ownership, not being co-opted by tech.

**What they see right now:**
- Explicit Transition Towns comparison (section 06) — flattering, shows knowledge of the movement
- The Build → Document → Replicate flywheel mirrors the Transition model (handbook → local adoption)
- Cooperative ownership structure (SC)
- Open licensing (AGPL + CC BY-SA)
- "City by city" language maps directly to Transition logic

**What's missing — the hard blockers:**
1. **No connection to existing Transition infrastructure.** The site *references* Transition Towns but doesn't link to any existing Transition group, hasn't partnered with any, and doesn't explain how GH fits into the existing network. Rob would ask: "Is this a complement to what we do, or a parallel effort that ignores us?"
2. **No clear community governance story.** The cooperative structure is mentioned but abstract. How do actual communities participate? What happens when two people disagree about a feature? What are the decision-making processes? Transition people are extremely governance-literate and will find the current description thin.
3. **"AI-assisted development" framing is risky for this audience.** Many Transition people are cautious or hostile toward AI. The hero subtitle centers AI. Section 02 leads with AI. The site could easily trigger a "this is tech-solutionism dressed up as community empowerment" reaction.
4. **No mention of ecological cost.** Transition is a post-carbon movement. AI computation has a significant energy footprint. The site mentions green data infrastructure (Leafcloud, Qarnot) but never addresses GH's own ecological impact or values around technology and energy use.
5. **The learn page is promising but empty.** Transition people will go straight to "Learn" looking for playbooks and workshop formats. Right now everything says "Coming soon." If Rob visited today, he'd see the *intention* but not the substance.

**What would make them say "hell yes":**
- A clear paragraph in the blueprint section (06) explaining how GH complements (doesn't replace) Transition work: "Transition Towns gave communities food, energy, and resilience. GH adds the digital layer."
- Governance documentation: even a brief "how decisions get made" page
- Acknowledgment of the ecological dimension: GH's position on computation, energy, server choices
- One completed playbook or workshop template — even a draft — proves the model
- An invitation specifically for Transition groups: "If you run a Transition Town, here's how GH can help your community."

## Audience 4: Event Organizers & Conference Curators

**Who they are:** People programming FOSDEM, Mozfest, Open Belgium, Smart City Expo, TEDx Brussels, Hackers & Founders, local meetup organizers. They need: a clear speaker topic, an engaging angle, evidence the talk will draw an audience, a credible speaker.

**What they see right now:**
- A strong narrative arc (the literacy analogy, the bakery anecdote, the name etymology)
- Concrete examples (PostVisit.ai, TARA, CrossBeam)
- A live hackathon — live events are gold for conference content
- A Belgian founder with a personal story (shop owner who codes)

**What's missing — the hard blockers:**
1. **No speaker page or "Book Julien" section.** Event organizers can't book someone they can't evaluate. There's no photo, no bio, no speaking topics, no previous talk links, no "I'm available for talks" signal anywhere.
2. **No talk topics offered.** The site contains material for at least 5 different talks (the cost collapse, citizen coding, cooperative software, the Transition parallel, the hackathon story). But none of these are packaged as "here's a talk you can book."
3. **No media assets.** No headshot, no logo, no press kit. Event organizers need these to put someone in a program.
4. **The hackathon is a perfect content piece but undersold.** "Solo founder builds two tools for his shop in five days" is a compelling conference story. But the hackathon page doesn't position itself as talk material — it's just a project page.

**What would make them say "hell yes":**
- A "Speaking" or "Press" page with: bio, headshot, 3-4 talk titles with one-line descriptions, previous appearances, and a booking email
- A press kit (logo, brand assets, one-pager PDF)
- The hackathon devlog positioned as "live build documentary" — link to it from a speaking page as proof of the narrative

## Audience 5: Open-Source Community & Developers

**Who they are:** People on Hacker News, GitHub, Lobste.rs, Mastodon tech circles, FOSDEM attendees, cooperative tech folks (CoTech, Platform Cooperativism). They care about: code quality, license choice, governance, architecture, whether this is vapor or real.

**What they see right now:**
- AGPL-3.0 — strong signal (copyleft, serious intent)
- GitHub links to repos
- Technical stack hints (ActivityPub, browser-first, POS data)
- A hackathon with a dev log (good credibility builder)

**What's missing — the hard blockers:**
1. **No technical architecture documentation accessible from the site.** Devs want to see: how is this built? What stack? What's the repo structure? The SPEC link goes to a manifesto document, not technical docs.
2. **No contributing guide.** "Fork & contribute" is a CTA but there's no CONTRIBUTING.md, no good-first-issues, no development setup guide linked from the site.
3. **No code activity visible.** Is the GitHub active? When was the last commit? How many contributors? The site doesn't surface any of this. A dev who clicks through to a dead repo will bounce immediately.
4. **The site's tone is too motivational for devs.** The literacy analogy, the Rilke quote, the "eyes light up" CTA — this reads as marketing, not engineering. Devs are allergic to hype and will pattern-match this to "another manifesto project that never ships."
5. **No API/integration documentation.** For David Toolkit specifically — how does the POS integration work? What data formats? Can I plug in my own POS system? This is what devs actually want.

**What would make them say "hell yes":**
- A "For Developers" page or section: architecture overview, tech stack, how to set up locally, how to contribute
- Active GitHub repos with recent commits, clear READMEs, open issues tagged "good first issue"
- A technical blog / devlog (the hackathon fills this gap — make sure it's discoverable from GitHub)
- Link to CI/CD, test coverage, deployment docs

## Audience 6: Shop Owners & Small Business Owners

**Who they are:** Julien's actual peers. People running specialty food shops, bakeries, cafés, small retailers. Not technical. They care about: will this actually help my business? Is it easy? Is it free? Can I see it working?

**What they see right now:**
- A very long, philosophical website about software movements
- Some mention of David Toolkit and Chez Julien (their world)
- A hackathon about sales prediction and workflow maps (directly relevant)
- Lots of jargon: AGPL, ActivityPub, federation, cooperative SC, forks

**What's missing — the hard blockers:**
1. **The site is not written for them.** The language is for investors, tech people, and movement thinkers. A shop owner reading "AI-assisted development crossed a threshold" will stop reading. They need: "We build free tools that help small shops compete with big chains."
2. **No demo or screenshot of David Toolkit.** They can't see what the tool looks like or does. The David Toolkit page links to a GitHub repo — that's a dead end for a non-technical person.
3. **No "how do I get this for my shop?" path.** The CTA is "email Julien." But what happens after? Is there a setup process? Do I need to install something? How long does it take? What data do I need?
4. **The workflow map exists but is buried.** There's an existing chez-julien-workflow-map.html page linked once in the demos section. This is the most tangible, shop-owner-relevant piece of the whole site — and it's nearly invisible.
5. **No before/after story.** "Julien built tools for his shop" — but what changed? Does he waste less food? Order more accurately? Save time? Shop owners want results, not philosophy.

**What would make them say "hell yes":**
- A dedicated "For Shop Owners" landing page: plain language, screenshots, a step-by-step "how to get started" path
- A working demo of David Toolkit (even a static screenshot walkthrough)
- A before/after story with real numbers: "Before: I threw away €X of cheese per week. After: waste dropped by Y%."
- The workflow map promoted as a standalone tool
- A "join the next hackathon" or "get a free tool assessment" offer

## Cross-Cutting Issues (Affect All Audiences)

1. **No About/Team page.** Every audience wants to know who's behind this. One person? A team? What's their background? No photos, no bios, no LinkedIn links anywhere.

2. **No clear site navigation.** The main page is a long scroll with no header nav. Top bar only has demo links. New pages (Learn, Hackathon) are in the nav but there's no persistent menu across all pages. Each audience arrives and has to scroll the entire manifesto to find what's relevant to them.

3. **No differentiated entry points.** Everyone gets the same long-scroll experience. An investor and a shop owner have completely different needs but land on the same page. There's no "I'm a [X], take me to [Y]" routing.

4. **"Coming soon" everywhere.** The learn page has 5+ "coming soon" items. The workshop formats say "Launching Q2/Q3 2026." The governance docs don't exist. The contributing guide doesn't exist. Every audience hits a wall of promises.

5. **No social proof aggregation.** GitHub stars, community members, newsletter subscribers, event attendees — whatever numbers exist (even small ones) should be visible. Zero numbers anywhere = zero credibility for anyone evaluating traction.

6. **The Rilke quote and some sections feel indulgent.** Every audience except maybe Rob Hopkins will see the Rilke quote as noise. Same for the extended name etymology (4 cards for why it's called Grassroots Hopper). These dilute the pitch.
