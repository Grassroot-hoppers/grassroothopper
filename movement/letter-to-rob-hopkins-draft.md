# Letter to Rob Hopkins — Draft

*Subject line: Can we talk?*

---

Hello Rob,

I might be your biggest fan. I know everyone says that, but let me make my case.

A few years ago I saw you at a conference for your book *What If?*. I read it with absolute awe. You became my inspiration. That day I decided I would do everything I can to become someone like you. Meeting you in real life made something click — something I had always felt but never fully believed. That a life of creativity in the service of humanity is possible, needed, and genuinely fun.

I spent the last eight years continuing my life — running a specialty food shop in Brussels, dancing Lindy Hop, being part of my community — while always keeping something in the back of my head: *one day I will have an idea, and a movement, just like yours.*

That day has arrived.

---

I'm contacting you to proudly present my ideas, in the hope that I can lure you into a conversation on this subject. What I want from you: help me understand what it takes to create a movement. A few tips you probably learned the hard way. What I can offer you in return: I can teach you something about what just happened to the world. Something that is very well documented and researched, sometimes compiled under the headline: *What the fuck happened in 2025?*

Here's what happened. The gap between having an idea and coding software has collapsed. This is the most revolutionary thing that happened this decade.

Let me use an analogy. Think about literacy. Imagine we are in the 1960s. Most people still can't read. Can't sign their name. The few who can read and write use the law as a shield to impose their will on the rest. Then — all at once — access to reading and writing is given to everyone. Everything changes. The power structures built on information asymmetry crumble.

That is the same analogy we are living through right now with software. Until yesterday, you had to trust software developers and engineers to build your daily tools. As a retail business owner, that means my point-of-sale system, my admin system, my payment processing — all of it costs an outrageous amount of money and none of it fits my specific needs. I am dependent on companies who don't know my shop, my customers, or my constraints.

That era is over.

I am now building all the software I need by myself, during my lunch hours, on my phone. I have spent the last four months designing software for my own shop. It is extremely fun and I am astonished by the quality of what I can produce. The technology I'm using is four months old. I started using it three months ago, at a pace of four to eight hours per day. I consider myself a pioneer on this subject.

My theory — and I believe it deeply — is that the future is already here but not yet widely distributed. When it reaches normal people, it will enable them to have far more impact on their communities than they ever could before.

---

Hobbyist coders will someday represent 10 to 15% of the population. Let me give you a picture of what that looks like.

One of these people walks into their favorite bakery. "You don't have a system for demand prediction?" The baker says: "No, that's reserved for the big chains." The hobbyist coder says: "I can spend a weekend building one for you."

Rob, I can tell you — it only took me a weekend. Today. Imagine what it will look like in six months, one year, two years.

---

I am starting a movement. I call it **Grassroot Hopper**.

The name is a blend of everything I care about:

- **Grassroot** — bottom-up, citizen-driven, not corporate.
- **Grace Hopper** — the first coder who tried to democratize programming.
- **Hopping** — the architecture of what I do: hopping from open-source project to open-source project, taking the pieces you need to assemble your own design.
- **Hop** — the crop native to Belgium. We used it to brew beer that is now vastly imported from the US. It reflects exactly what happened with our social media and software: built elsewhere, imported to us, designed for someone else's profit. It's now time to custom-build them in our own towns and cities.

My prediction is that in a few years, people will own their own social networks. Normal people will use tools designed by them, for them. The synergy between open-source projects and AI-assisted coding — which is extraordinarily good at learning from existing projects and reproducing them with modifications — means that the more open-source projects arrive, the easier it becomes to duplicate, modify, and tailor them to your needs.

---

I'm already building two things.

**David Retail** is a set of open-source tools for independent shop owners — prediction, inventory management, point of sale. I'm creating the tools I need for my own shop today, and publishing them online so any other retail owner can open them, read the documentation, modify them to their own needs, then upload the improved version back to the internet. A free circle of iteration, without any engineering teams. Creating a feedback loop that feels like being part of a movement of solution-makers.

**Social Media V2** is a cooperative social network launching with the Lindy Hop community in Brussels. No likes, no comments, no algorithm. You see what the people around you are creating. If you want to tell them it's amazing, you tell them in person.

A social network and retail tools are only the humble beginning. I believe the citizen-builders of tomorrow might be the most important actors of our future. Software companies built on armies of engineers optimizing sales may not survive the simple, open-source designs — cheap, efficient, highly customizable — that will flood the internet. Caring and being willing to spend time solving problems will become much more powerful than having a lot of money and a team of engineers.

---

I recognize something in what you built with Transition Towns. You didn't build a platform. You named an identity, wrote a handbook, and let 1,400+ communities self-organize around it. That's exactly what I want to do — but for digital infrastructure instead of energy and food.

My goal is to federalize and name a movement, instead of having millions of people trying to do their own thing in their own way without coordination. The Grassroot Hopper movement is about providing documentation, guidance, and community to everybody in the world who wakes up tomorrow wanting to create software that solves problems and improves the lives of their own communities.

---

I would love to meet you. I have a concrete proposal for our conversation.

The best way I've found to teach these new tools — and I've done it with my employees and with my 12-year-old nephew — is to build something together. Specifically: a video game.

Here's my pitch: we sit down together and create a strategy game about trying to start a Transition Towns movement in your town. A decision-based strategy game with random events, difficulty levels, and different play styles. We can build it in a few hours. You'd leave our conversation with a working game on your phone and a visceral understanding of how fast ideas can become software now.

Or we build something else entirely. Whatever excites you. The point is the experience.

---

Rob, your movement inspired mine. I would be honored to share a conversation with you.

Warm regards,
Julien
